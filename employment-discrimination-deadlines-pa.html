Workplace discrimination timing should be checked early.

Workplace discrimination concerns can involve internal HR steps, Pennsylvania Human Relations Commission filings, federal Equal Employment Opportunity Commission charges, and civil claims. The timing can depend on what happened, when it happened, who the employer is, what category is involved and whether any agency documents already exist. This page is a starting point, not legal advice for a specific situation.

Deadline awareness

Agency filing windows may be short.

Employment discrimination and retaliation concerns may involve HR materials, PHRC filings, EEOC charges, union or grievance procedures, public-employment rules, accommodation requests, termination dates, discipline, harassment, pay issues or other workplace events.

Some agency filing windows can be short and may be measured from a specific act, report, denial, discipline, termination or other dated event. The correct timing may depend on the employer, protected category, agency, event date and procedural history.

Do not assume that an internal HR complaint, union process, employer investigation, mediation or informal discussion pauses an outside agency or court deadline. Because timing can affect available options, preserve the record and ask for legal review promptly.

A helpful preparation step is to build a dated timeline. Include the workplace event, report to management or HR, employer response, discipline or termination, accommodation request and any agency filing or notice.

Do not assume HR pauses outside filing windows.

Internal HR processes generally do not pause external agency or court filing windows. If an outside filing window might be running, confirm the timing directly with an attorney rather than assuming the internal process protects the outside window.

Timeline to prepare

  • Date of the workplace event or conduct.
  • Date reported to HR, management, a supervisor, union, or agency.
  • Date of any employer response.
  • Date of discipline, termination, denial, schedule change, demotion, pay change, or other adverse action.
  • Date any agency document was filed or received.

What to preserve

Organize the proof before details fade.

A clear record helps sort whether the concern is an internal workplace issue, an agency filing issue, a civil claim or another legal path.

Written communications

Save messages, text messages, HR portal messages, complaint forms, accommodation requests, discipline, evaluations, schedules, pay stubs, termination letters, policies, and employer responses with visible dates. Keep lawful copies outside employer systems where permitted. Do not delete the originals after copying.

Employment documents

Keep offer letters, job descriptions, schedules, pay stubs, handbook pages, discipline records, evaluations, attendance logs and termination papers. Identify which documents came from the employer and when they were received.

Retaliation and discrimination timeline

A useful timeline should separate the protected concern from the employer action. Include dates for the workplace event, report to management or HR, employer response, adverse action, witness involvement, agency filings, letters, portal messages, and any right-to-sue communication.

Accommodation and leave documents

If the issue involves a disability or religious accommodation request, parental or medical leave, or pregnancy-related accommodation, save the request itself, the employer's response, any supporting documentation referenced and any policy excerpts the employer cited.

Witness and HR materials

List people with first-hand knowledge and describe what each person saw, heard, received, sent, or decided. Identify whether each person is a supervisor, HR employee, coworker, former employee, union representative, customer, vendor, or third party. Do not contact witnesses in a way that could create workplace issues or be characterized as pressure.

Agency documents and envelopes

If PHRC or EEOC documents have already been filed or received, save the charge, notice, response dates, right-to-sue letters, mailed envelopes, and portal messages. The mailing date or portal date may affect the upcoming date.

Before contacting the office

Before you contact the office

Before contacting the office, avoid deleting documents, changing originals, posting sensitive details publicly, contacting witnesses in a way that could create workplace issues, or assuming that an internal process protects an outside filing date.

Keep the first conversation focused on basics: employer name, work location, event dates, agency papers, timing notices and the type of workplace issue. Keep confidential details out of the initial message until conflict review is complete. Deadline FAQ

Common employment timing questions.

Does an internal HR complaint protect my outside filing date?

Not necessarily. Internal procedures and external agency filing dates are generally separate. Confirm timing with an attorney rather than assuming one process pauses the other.

What if more than one event happened?

List every event with its date. Some timing rules may run from specific acts, and a lawyer generally needs the full timeline to evaluate which dates may matter.

Should I keep personal notes?

A dated timeline can help you remember events, but preserve originals as well. Personal notes are generally not a substitute for messages, texts, notices, policies, evaluations, pay stubs, or agency documents.

What if I already received a right-to-sue letter?

A right-to-sue letter may create its own filing date. The letter itself should be reviewed promptly, along with the envelope or portal notice if available.

Does this page cover wage, contract, non-compete, or trade-secret claims?

No. Wage payment, overtime, contract, non-compete, and trade-secret disputes can follow their own rules and limitation periods. If those documents are part of the same workplace situation, bring them too.

A note about general information

Every situation depends on the facts.

These notes can help you understand the issue, but they are not legal advice for any specific matter.

PHRC and EEOC windows

Public PHRC and EEOC guidance identifies short filing windows for many discrimination matters. The exact claim, employer, forum, and event date control.

Event-by-event timing

Employment-discrimination timing can attach to each separate event. Preserve the dated record of each discipline, denial, harassment incident, termination, or agency filing.
