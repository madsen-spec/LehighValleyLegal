What to expect

A first call is not the same as representation.

The first step is a short, practical exchange so the office can understand timing and whether the issue can be discussed.

  1. 1

    Give a short description.

    Share your name, contact information, the kind of problem, where it is pending and whether anything is scheduled.

  2. 2

    Identify the source of the problem.

    A charge, order, citation, notice, letter, contract or agency message can help explain what needs attention.

  3. 3

    Wait before sharing sensitive facts.

    The office must determine whether the issue can be discussed before detailed confidential information is shared.

  4. 4

    Confirm scope before relying on advice.

    If the matter is accepted, the attorney and client must complete engagement terms before representation begins.

Frequently asked questions

Common questions.

I received a court notice. What should I do first?

Read it for the county, court, date, time and type of case. If there is a docket number, agency notice or PennDOT letter, mention that when contacting the office.

How do I know which practice area fits my issue?

Focus on what happened. An arrest, family-court order, agency notice, insurance letter, workplace decision or contract dispute often points to the closest page.

Can I send confidential facts right away?

No. A short description is enough for scheduling. Save confidential details until the office confirms that the issue can be discussed.

What information is helpful at first?

The county or court, the kind of issue, any scheduled date, the people involved and the document or event that caused the problem.

Does contacting the office create an attorney-client relationship?

No. An attorney-client relationship is created only after the office accepts the matter and engagement terms are completed.

Can anyone promise a specific result?

No. Legal outcomes depend on the facts, law, procedure, evidence, the court or agency involved and decisions made by courts, agencies, opposing parties or insurers.
