Focus on the family picture and the court status.

Divorce can involve court filings, service, finances, property, debt, support, parenting schedules and settlement discussions. The first task is understanding what has been filed, what is scheduled, what financial information exists and what is actually disputed.

A divorce conversation should cover the county, docket status if any, current living arrangements, parenting matters, income, property, debts and any pending support or custody case.

County procedure, service matters and court scheduling may affect the next step. If a case has already been filed, the complaint, docket number, proof of service, notices, orders and court correspondence show where things stand. If no case has been filed, the first conversation should still identify where the parties live, where the property and records are located, and whether any related custody, support, PFA or civil case is already active.

Information that may matter

  • divorce papers, service papers, and court notices;
  • current custody, support, PFA, or related family court orders;
  • pay stubs, tax returns, benefits, account statements, debts, mortgage, lease, and insurance information;
  • deeds, vehicle titles, retirement account statements, business records, and loan documents;
  • messages or agreements about property, support, parenting time, household expenses, housing or insurance;
  • records showing major assets, debts, separate property claims, gifts, inheritances, business interests or retirement accounts if those matters may be disputed.

Do not assume the pace.

Divorce timing depends on filings, service, agreements, contested matters, financial disclosures, court scheduling, and related custody or support matters.

Divorce and parenting overlap

Property, support and custody questions often overlap.

A divorce consultation may involve more than the divorce filing itself. Clients often need to discuss parenting schedules, support, marital property, debts, housing, insurance, retirement accounts, tax documents, and whether any immediate court date is already scheduled.

Income, expenses, assets, liabilities and parenting arrangements often drive the practical conversation. If you have already received a court filing, keep every page, including envelopes and service information.

If custody or support is already active in another docket, identify that case number, current order, conference notice, wage information, childcare matter and any proposed schedule or expenses. Related family-court matters may affect what should be handled first.

Filing and service status

Identify whether a divorce complaint has been filed, whether the other party has been served, whether any counterclaim or response has been received, and whether the court has issued a notice or scheduling order. If service is disputed or incomplete, bring every mailing, envelope, sheriff return, acceptance, affidavit or communication about delivery.

Financial picture

Divorce planning is clearer when income, expenses, assets and debts are understood before settlement discussions begin. Pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, credit-card statements, mortgage information, retirement statements, insurance information, vehicle information, business interests and recurring household expenses may all matter.

Property and debt questions

Make a practical list of real estate, vehicles, accounts, retirement assets, personal property, business interests, loans, credit cards and tax obligations. Note when each item was acquired, whose name appears on the account or title, whether payments are current, and whether there are papers showing value or ownership.

Communication and agreements

Save written communications about separation, support, custody, property, bills, housing, insurance, accounts and settlement proposals. Do not edit original messages. A dated timeline can help, but original messages often matter more than a summary.

Before contacting our office

Know the county, docket number if any, filing and service status, scheduled court event, current orders, involved party names and urgent dates. Keep confidential facts out of the initial message until we confirm it can discuss the matter.

Separate the marriage case from the money questions.

Divorce paperwork, support, custody and property division can move on different tracks. Treating them as one pile makes the first meeting less useful.

James's view: A fast divorce decree is not always the right goal if property, retirement or support claims have not been preserved.

What controls the next step

Pennsylvania divorce grounds appear in 23 Pa.C.S.A. § 3301, and equitable distribution is governed by 23 Pa.C.S.A. § 3502.

Northampton County divorce cases may involve pleadings, discovery, support proceedings and a Master's process before final resolution.
