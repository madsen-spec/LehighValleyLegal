Court and license tracks

DUI defense should account for both tracks.

Most DUI calls involve two worries at once: what happens in court and what happens to the driver's license. ARD questions, insurance effects, employment impact, treatment requirements, CDL status and an upcoming court date may move on different schedules.

The criminal case may involve charges, bail, arraignment, a preliminary hearing, ARD review, plea discussions, motions or trial matters. The license side may involve PennDOT notices, refusal matters, restoration requirements, suspension timing, ignition interlock or limited-license questions. The court track and license track should be reviewed together, not in isolation.

A DUI review should account for the charges, test or refusal matter, next hearing date, PennDOT notices, current license status and practical choices that may affect the case. A missing notice may change the timeline.

Do not wait on license mail.

If a PennDOT notice arrives, keep the envelope and prepare the date, reason for suspension and any restoration or appeal instructions.

Helpful first details

  • Date, county, and location of the stop.
  • Charges, citation numbers, and next court date.
  • Blood, breath, lab, hospital or refusal matter.
  • ARD application materials or county program instructions, if started.
  • PennDOT suspension, restoration, interlock, or limited-license correspondence and envelopes.
  • Prior DUI, license or traffic history if it may affect the matter.
  • Work, school, childcare, military, CDL, company-vehicle, delivery, rideshare, or medical-driving concerns.

ARD and resolution questions

ARD questions depend on the charge, facts, prior record, county practice, timing, license history and program requirements. Do not assume eligibility or ineligibility from a general online summary. Application instructions, criminal-history concerns, treatment or evaluation matters and court notices may all matter.

Testing and refusal matters

Testing and refusal matters should be checked before conclusions are drawn. Blood, breath, hospital, lab and refusal information may affect both the criminal case and driver-license consequences. Keep chemical-test warnings, lab reports, officer documents, hospital documents, PennDOT letters and envelopes.

Ignition interlock and limited-license questions

Some DUI-related suspensions can involve ignition interlock requirements or limited-license questions. Eligibility, timing and conditions can vary and should be checked against the specific PennDOT notice and driving history rather than assumed from a general online summary.

Work, CDL, and daily driving concerns

If driving affects work, school, childcare, medical care, military obligations, a company vehicle, commercial driving, delivery work, rideshare work or professional licensing, identify those concerns during the first conversation. Collateral consequences can matter even when the court case appears straightforward.

Why timing matters

The safest first step is to identify each important date: the court date, mailing date, PennDOT date, suspension date, restoration date, ARD date, appeal date or evaluation deadline. Do not assume the court case and license matter share the same timing.

Notices to keep, even if you are unsure

Save notices from the incident, the testing process, the court system and PennDOT, including envelopes and online messages. Some letters look unimportant until the timing matter becomes clear.
