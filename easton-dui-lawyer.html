Court and license tracks

DUI defense should account for both tracks.

Most DUI calls involve two worries at once: what happens in court and what happens to the driver's license. ARD questions, insurance effects, employment impact, treatment requirements, CDL status and an upcoming court date can all move on different schedules.

The criminal case may involve charges, bail, arraignment, a preliminary hearing, ARD evaluation, plea discussions, motions, or trial preparation. The license side may involve PennDOT notices, refusal-related issues, restoration requirements, suspension timing, ignition interlock, or limited-license questions. The court track and license track should be reviewed together, not in isolation.

A useful DUI review starts by sorting the charges, chemical-testing or refusal documents, next hearing date, PennDOT notices, current license status and practical choices that may affect the case. Bring every paper, including envelopes, because one missing notice can change the timeline.

Do not wait on license mail.

If a PennDOT notice arrives, keep the envelope and prepare the date, reason for suspension and any restoration or appeal instructions.

Useful first-call details

  • Date, county, and location of the stop.
  • Charges, citation numbers, and next court date.
  • Blood, breath, lab, hospital, or refusal documents.
  • ARD application materials or county program instructions, if started.
  • PennDOT suspension, restoration, interlock, or limited-license correspondence and envelopes.
  • Prior DUI, license, or traffic documents if it may affect the matter.
  • Work, school, childcare, military, CDL, company-vehicle, delivery, rideshare, or medical-driving concerns.

ARD and resolution questions

ARD questions depend on the charge, facts, prior record, county practice, timing, license history, and program requirements. Do not assume eligibility or ineligibility from a general online summary. Bring any ARD documents, application instructions, criminal-history concerns, treatment or evaluation documents, and court notices.

Testing and refusal documents

Testing and refusal documents should be checked before conclusions are drawn. Blood, breath, hospital, lab, and refusal documents may affect both the criminal case and driver-license consequences. Keep chemical-test warnings, lab reports, officer documents, hospital documents, PennDOT letters, and envelopes.

Ignition interlock and limited-license questions

Some DUI-related suspensions can involve ignition interlock requirements or limited-license questions. Eligibility, timing, and conditions vary and should be checked against the specific PennDOT documents and driving history rather than assumed from a general online summary.

Work, CDL, and daily driving concerns

If driving affects work, school, childcare, medical care, military obligations, a company vehicle, commercial driving, delivery work, rideshare work, or professional licensing, identify those concerns during the first conversation. Collateral consequences can matter even when the court documents appear straightforward.

Why timing matters

The safest first step is to identify every date on every document: the court date, mailing date, PennDOT date, suspension date, restoration date, ARD date, appeal date, or evaluation deadline. Do not assume the court case and the license documents share the same deadline.

Documents to keep, even if you are unsure

Save every record from the incident, the testing process, the court system and PennDOT, including envelopes and online messages. Some documents look unimportant until a deadline reveals what they were timing. If a document looks duplicative, keep both copies.
