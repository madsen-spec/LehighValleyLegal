Estate planning

Estate planning for the people and decisions that matter.

Estate planning should make it clear who can act, who should receive property and what should happen if illness, incapacity or death creates hard decisions.

Focus on the people, property and choices involved.

Estate planning may involve wills, financial powers of attorney, health-care powers of attorney, living wills or advance directives, beneficiary designations, deeds, family roles and practical questions about who can act if someone cannot manage decisions. The discussion is clearer when basic family, property, account, health-care and existing-plan information is known.

Estate-planning needs vary by family structure, property, account ownership, health-care concerns, prior planning documents, life changes and applicable law. This page does not provide tax advice, asset-protection advice, Medicaid planning advice, probate advice or a promise that a particular document will fit every situation.

If someone has recently died, if there is already a court filing, if property must be sold soon or if a person may lack capacity to sign, identify that matter when contacting our office. Those facts can change what should be addressed first.

Information that may matter

  • existing wills, codicils, trusts, powers of attorney, living wills, health-care directives, prior estate plans and letters of instruction;
  • deeds, mortgage information, leases, tax bills, vehicle titles, business records, buy-sell agreements and closely held company documents;
  • bank, investment, retirement, life-insurance and other account records, including beneficiary forms and payable-on-death or transfer-on-death designations;
  • family information for spouses, children, stepchildren, dependents, estranged relatives, prior marriages and anyone who may need special consideration;
  • names and contact information for people who may act as executor, trustee, financial agent, health-care agent, guardian, backup fiduciary or practical helper;
  • questions about debts, taxes, health needs, digital accounts, pets, household responsibilities, business succession, real estate access and personal property.

Planning is not one-size-fits-all.

Do not assume an old will, form power of attorney, beneficiary form or deed still matches the current family, property and decision-maker situation.

Existing plans and life changes

Old plans can become a problem when life changes.

Prior wills, powers of attorney, health-care directives, beneficiary forms, deeds, account ownership, divorce decrees, support orders, business interests, tax matters and property information may affect the planning conversation.

Existing plans should be revisited after major life changes such as marriage, divorce, separation, birth or adoption of a child, death of a named fiduciary or beneficiary, a move, a new diagnosis, a new business interest, a real-estate change, a significant account change or a family conflict.

If the matter involves a closing date, real-estate transaction, family dispute, incapacity concern, court filing, recent death or immediate health-care decision, identify that matter when contacting our office.

Family and beneficiary information

Think through close family members, dependents, intended beneficiaries, people who should not receive authority or property, and anyone whose circumstances may require extra care. Include current names, contact information, relationships, ages of minor children and whether any person has special needs, creditor matters, substance-use concerns, estrangement or conflict with other family members.

Fiduciary and backup choices

Think through who could serve as executor, trustee, financial agent, health-care agent, guardian or backup decision-maker. It helps to know whether those people are willing, local enough to help, financially responsible, able to communicate with family members and likely to follow the client's instructions.

Real estate and business interests

Real estate, family businesses and closely held interests often require more than a simple list of assets. Deeds, mortgages, leases, tax bills, entity records, operating agreements, buy-sell agreements, business debt and access to property or business information may all matter.

Accounts and beneficiary designations

Account titles and beneficiary designations may affect whether property passes under a will or outside a will. Retirement-plan information, life-insurance information, annuity information, bank accounts and beneficiary forms may all matter.

Health-care and incapacity planning

Existing health-care powers of attorney, living wills, advance directives, POLST or DNR documents, physician or facility information and preferred decision-makers may matter. Also identify any immediate capacity concern, pending medical procedure, long-term care matter or family disagreement about health decisions.

Before you call

For first contact, identify the county, client name, family structure, urgent timing matter, existing-plan status and basic planning concern. Keep confidential family or financial details out of the initial message until we confirm that it can discuss the matter.

Estate planning

A will is only one part of the plan.

Estate planning should answer who can act during life, who receives property after death and which records make the plan work.

James's view: A simple will can be enough for some families. For others, beneficiary designations, powers of attorney and real estate planning matter more.

What controls the next step

Pennsylvania estate administration commonly involves Register of Wills filings and inheritance-tax planning by relationship category.

Northampton County estate matters may involve the Register of Wills and local real-estate records.

Next: Bring existing wills, powers of attorney, deeds, account statements, beneficiary forms and names of proposed fiduciaries.
