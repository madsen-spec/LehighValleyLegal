Start with the people, property and choices involved.

Estate planning may involve wills, financial powers of attorney, health-care powers of attorney, living wills or advance directives, beneficiary designations, deeds, family roles and practical questions about who can act if someone cannot manage decisions. The call is easier when basic family, property, account, health-care and existing-plan information is organized.

Estate-planning needs vary by family structure, property, account ownership, health-care concerns, prior papers, life changes and applicable law. This page does not provide tax advice, asset-protection advice, Medicaid planning advice, probate advice or a promise that a particular document will fit every situation.

If someone has recently died, if there is already a court filing, if property must be sold soon, or if a person may lack capacity to sign papers, identify that issue when contacting the office. Those facts can change what should be read first.

Information to gather

  • existing wills, codicils, trusts, powers of attorney, living wills, health-care directives, prior estate plans and letters of instruction;
  • deeds, mortgage information, leases, tax bills, vehicle titles, business records, buy-sell agreements and closely held company documents;
  • bank, investment, retirement, life-insurance and other account records, including beneficiary forms and payable-on-death or transfer-on-death designations;
  • family information for spouses, children, stepchildren, dependents, estranged relatives, prior marriages and anyone who may need special consideration;
  • names and contact information for people who may act as executor, trustee, financial agent, health-care agent, guardian, backup fiduciary or practical helper;
  • questions about debts, taxes, health needs, digital accounts, pets, household responsibilities, business succession, real estate access and personal property.

Planning is not one-size-fits-all.

Do not assume an old will, form power of attorney, beneficiary form or deed still matches the current family, property and decision-maker situation.

Planning records

Look at existing papers before replacing them.

Prior wills, powers of attorney, health-care directives, beneficiary forms, deeds, account papers, divorce decrees, support orders, business records, tax records and property records may affect the planning conversation. Bring copies rather than relying on memory.

Existing papers should be revisited after major life changes such as marriage, divorce, separation, birth or adoption of a child, death of a named fiduciary or beneficiary, a move, a new diagnosis, a new business interest, a real estate change, a significant account change or a family conflict.

If the issue involves a closing date, real estate transaction, family dispute, incapacity concern, court paper, recent death or immediate health-care decision, identify that issue when contacting the office.

Family and beneficiary information

Prepare a list of close family members, dependents, intended beneficiaries, people who should not receive authority or property, and anyone whose circumstances may require extra care. Include current names, contact information, relationships, ages of minor children and whether any person has special needs, creditor issues, substance-use concerns, estrangement or conflict with other family members.

Fiduciary and backup choices

Think through who could serve as executor, trustee, financial agent, health-care agent, guardian or backup decision-maker. A useful first call should identify whether those people are willing, local enough to help, financially responsible, able to communicate with family members and likely to follow the client's instructions.

Real estate and business interests

Bring deeds, mortgages, leases, tax bills, entity records, operating agreements, buy-sell agreements, business debt documents and information about who has access to the property or business records. Real estate, family businesses and closely held interests often require more than a simple list of assets.

Accounts and beneficiary designations

Gather account statements, retirement-plan information, life-insurance papers, annuity records, bank records and beneficiary forms. Account titles and beneficiary designations may affect whether property passes under a will or outside a will, so the actual forms and account ownership details matter.

Health-care and incapacity planning

Bring existing health-care powers of attorney, living wills, advance directives, POLST or DNR documents if any exist, physician or facility documents, and notes about preferred decision-makers. Also identify any immediate capacity concern, pending medical procedure, long-term care issue or family disagreement about health decisions.

Before you call

Have the county, client name, family structure, urgent timing issue, existing-plan status, real estate location, major account types, preferred fiduciaries and any conflict or capacity concern ready. Keep confidential family or financial details out of the initial message until the office confirms that it can discuss the issue.

People, property, and powers

Estate-planning preparation should identify family relationships, fiduciary choices, real property, accounts, beneficiary forms, business interests, and existing documents.

No one-size-fits-all document

A will, power of attorney, health-care document, trust, deed, or beneficiary form can serve different functions. Document choice depends on facts and legal advice.
