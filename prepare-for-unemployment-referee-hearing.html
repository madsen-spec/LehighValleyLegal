How to prepare for an unemployment referee hearing.

A Pennsylvania unemployment referee hearing is usually focused on the matters listed in the notice and the evidence presented. Preparation should focus on the determination, appeal confirmation, hearing notice, listed matters, exhibits, witnesses and instructions for sending documents.

Before the hearing

Know what matter the referee is deciding.

The referee hearing notice is usually the roadmap. It may identify the legal matter, hearing date, hearing format, witness and exhibit instructions, and what to do if an interpreter, postponement or special arrangement is needed.

Unemployment appeal timing should be checked against the actual determination notice and current agency instructions. If timeliness is questioned, the hearing may address timing separately from whether benefits should be allowed or denied.

Organize the packet by date. A short timeline should include hire date, separation date, final incident or reason for leaving, UC filing date, determination date, appeal filing date and hearing date. Add a short note next to each exhibit explaining why it matters.

The hearing should be prepared around the listed matter. A voluntary quit case is not prepared the same way as a discharge case. A misconduct dispute is not prepared the same way as an availability matter or timeliness matter.

Bring the whole packet.

Do not bring only the page you think matters. Determinations, notices, envelopes and UC system messages can affect timing and hearing matters.

Common exhibits

  • Employer policies and handbook excerpts
  • Warnings, attendance logs and schedules
  • Texts, messages and resignation or termination letters
  • Medical, transportation or availability records if relevant

Preparation checklist

Four practical steps before the hearing.

This resource provides general information. The actual hearing plan depends on the notice, the determination and the facts.

1. Identify the matter

Look for the listed matter on the notice. Misconduct, voluntary quit, refusal of work, availability and appeal timeliness may require different proof. If more than one matter is listed, organize documents separately for each matter.

2. Sort documents by date

Put determinations, employer records, messages, policies and other exhibits in chronological order so the hearing record is easier to follow. Keep the original notice and envelope with the packet.

3. Choose witnesses carefully

First-hand witnesses are usually more useful than people repeating what they heard. Make sure witnesses understand the hearing date, format and specific topic they can address.

4. Follow exhibit instructions

The hearing notice may require exhibits to be sent before the hearing or shared with the other party. Follow the notice and keep proof that the documents were sent. Do not assume documents will be considered if they were not shared according to the notice instructions.

Referee hearing FAQ

Referee hearing questions.

Can I send more documents after the hearing?

Do not assume you can. The hearing notice should guide the process, and the record may close after the hearing unless the referee allows more information. Send documents according to the notice instructions when possible.

Should I read a prepared statement?

A short timeline can help, but the hearing usually turns on testimony and documents that answer the listed matter. Prepare to answer questions clearly and to explain specific dates and events.

What if the employer does not appear?

Attend anyway unless the referee or agency tells you otherwise. The referee may still take evidence and matter a decision. Bring your documents and be prepared to testify.

What if I receive an unfavorable referee decision?

Save the decision immediately. Further appeal rights may have separate deadlines and should be evaluated promptly using the decision date and instructions.

UC referee hearing

The referee hearing is the evidence hearing.

The UC referee usually decides the case from testimony and exhibits presented at the hearing. Treat the first hearing date as the real date.

James's view: Most people prepare too much argument and too little evidence. The referee needs admissible proof and clear testimony.
