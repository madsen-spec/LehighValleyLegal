How to prepare for an unemployment referee hearing.

A Pennsylvania unemployment referee hearing is a focused evidence hearing. Preparation should focus on the determination, appeal confirmation, hearing notice, listed issues, exhibits, witnesses and instructions for sending documents.

Quick reminder

  • Save every UC message and envelope.
  • Match documents to the listed issue.
  • Do not wait until the day before the hearing.

Before the hearing

Know what issue the referee is deciding.

The referee hearing notice is usually the roadmap. It may identify the legal issue, hearing date, hearing format, witness and exhibit instructions, and what to do if an interpreter, postponement or special arrangement is needed.

Pennsylvania unemployment guidance generally says appeals from determinations must be filed no later than 21 calendar days after the determination date. If timeliness is questioned, the hearing may address both whether the appeal was late and whether unemployment benefits should be allowed or denied.

Organize the packet by date. A short timeline should include hire date, separation date, final incident or reason for leaving, UC filing date, determination date, appeal filing date and hearing date. Add a short note next to each exhibit explaining why it matters.

The hearing should be prepared around the listed issue. A voluntary quit case is not prepared the same way as a discharge case. A misconduct dispute is not prepared the same way as an availability issue or timeliness issue.

Bring the whole packet.

Do not bring only the page you think matters. Determinations, notices, envelopes and UC system messages can affect timing and hearing issues.

Common exhibits

  • Employer policies and handbook excerpts
  • Warnings, attendance logs and schedules
  • Texts, messages and resignation or termination letters
  • Medical, transportation or availability records if relevant

Preparation checklist

Four practical steps before the hearing.

This resource provides general information. The actual hearing plan depends on the notice, the determination and the facts.

1. Identify the issue

Look for the listed issue on the notice. Misconduct, voluntary quit, refusal of work, availability and appeal timeliness require different proof. If more than one issue is listed, organize documents separately for each issue.

2. Sort documents by date

Put determinations, employer records, messages, policies and other exhibits in chronological order so the hearing record is easier to follow. Keep the original notice and envelope with the packet.

3. Choose witnesses carefully

First-hand witnesses are usually more useful than people repeating what they heard. Make sure witnesses understand the hearing date, format and specific topic they can address.

4. Follow exhibit instructions

The hearing notice may require exhibits to be sent before the hearing or shared with the other party. Follow the notice and keep proof that the documents were sent. Do not assume a referee will consider documents that were not shared according to the notice instructions.

Referee hearing FAQ

Referee hearing questions.

Can I send more documents after the hearing?

Do not assume you can. The hearing notice controls the process, and the record may close after the hearing unless the referee allows more information. Send documents according to the notice instructions when possible.

Should I read a prepared statement?

A short timeline can help, but the hearing usually turns on testimony and documents that answer the listed issue. Prepare to answer questions clearly and to explain specific dates and events.

What if the employer does not appear?

Attend anyway unless the referee or agency tells you otherwise. The referee may still take evidence and issue a decision. Bring your documents and be prepared to testify.

What if I receive an unfavorable referee decision?

Save the decision immediately. Further appeal rights have their own deadlines and should be evaluated promptly using the decision date and instructions.
