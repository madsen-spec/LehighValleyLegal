Estate planning

A good plan should match real life, not just a form.

A planning conversation should identify who should make decisions, who should receive property, what documents already exist and what assets may pass outside a will.

For Easton-area planning, deeds, account information, beneficiary designations, insurance documents, business interests and family circumstances can all change what documents make sense.

Document choices depend on facts

Wills, powers of attorney, health-care directions, trusts, and beneficiary designations serve different purposes. Do not assume one document covers every asset or decision.

Ownership and beneficiary details

Before drafting, confirm real-estate titles, account ownership, beneficiary forms, insurance documents and family relationships from current records.

Planning questions

Estate-planning questions.

What should I think about before discussing a will?

Think about who should receive property, who should handle responsibilities and whether any old documents or beneficiary forms need attention.

Do beneficiary forms matter?

Yes. Beneficiary forms may control certain accounts or policies, so they should be reviewed with the rest of the plan.

Can this page tell me which documents I need?

No. It explains what information may matter. Actual document choices depend on facts and legal advice.
