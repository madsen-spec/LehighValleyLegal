Focus on the court, charge and upcoming date.

Most criminal-defense calls involve a specific event: an arrest, summons, warrant, probation matter, police call, citation or preliminary hearing notice. The first discussion should identify the charge, court, upcoming date, bail status and immediate risk.

A first defense review may look at the complaint, affidavit if available, bail terms, witness matters, discovery, collateral concerns and realistic defense goals. The immediate task may be dealing with a preliminary hearing, responding to a warrant, evaluating a police request for a statement, protecting employment or licensing concerns, or deciding how to handle a scheduled court date.

For local cases, identify the arresting agency, magisterial district judge, Court of Common Pleas docket number if one exists, hearing notice, bail terms and anything from probation, police or the district attorney. If there are photos, videos, texts, social media messages, location information or witnesses, preserve them and say they exist.

Criminal defense strategy depends on more than whether the charge is a misdemeanor or felony. The facts, prior record, witnesses, search matters, statements, lab testing, video evidence, bail terms and collateral concerns can all affect the next step.

Before a preliminary hearing

Read the complaint, identify the alleged conduct, preserve available information, list witnesses and discuss whether contesting, waiving or negotiating the hearing fits the facts. If the affidavit is not available, ask how it can be obtained and whether any discovery has been provided.

After a warrant or missed court date

Confirm which court issued the warrant, why it was issued and what the next court step appears to be. Do not assume the problem will resolve without a court response. A missed hearing can affect bail, scheduling and the way the case proceeds.

When police want a statement

Before giving a statement, ask for legal advice. A conversation that seems informal may affect an investigation or prosecution.

When collateral consequences matter

Raise work, licensing, school, immigration, custody, housing, military or firearm concerns at the start so those matters are considered with the defense strategy. A criminal case may affect life outside the courtroom before final disposition.

What controls the next step
