Defense work

Early decisions can shape the case.

Criminal cases in Pennsylvania commonly begin with an alleged offense, police investigation, arrest, citation or criminal complaint. Early court events may include preliminary arraignment, bail conditions, a preliminary hearing or a scheduling order in the Court of Common Pleas. Those early events can shape the case even before formal discovery is complete.

The preliminary hearing stage is often the first meaningful point where the allegations, papers and available witnesses can be evaluated. Depending on the facts, the defense may need to consider whether to challenge the Commonwealth's evidence, preserve testimony, negotiate, address bail, request discovery, or waive the hearing for a strategic reason.

Before speaking with police, waiving a hearing, entering a plea or missing a court date, it is important to understand what the Commonwealth must prove, what information is missing and what practical options may be available.

A strong first defense conversation is practical: read the charges, identify the court date, check bail conditions, preserve urgent issues and decide what needs attention before the next appearance.

Local court preparation

Defense work starts with the paper trail.

In Easton-area criminal cases, the first conversation should identify the arresting agency, magisterial district judge, next hearing date, bail status, charges, discovery received and immediate collateral concerns. Organized records help an attorney spot factual disputes, missing information and timing issues that should not be missed.

If the case is already in the Northampton County Court of Common Pleas, bring the docket number, scheduling orders, plea documents, trial notices, sentencing papers and probation information. If the case is still before a magisterial district judge, bring the complaint, summons, affidavit if available, bail documents and preliminary hearing notice.

If there is a warrant, detainer or missed court date, do not assume the issue will resolve itself. The first question is procedural: which court issued the warrant, why it was issued, whether bail was forfeited or revoked, and what document explains the next step.

Preliminary hearings

The preliminary hearing is often the first major court event in a Pennsylvania criminal case. Preparation may include reviewing the complaint, identifying witness issues, preserving defenses, evaluating cross-examination, considering bail concerns and deciding whether contesting, waiving or negotiating fits the facts and client goals.

Warrants, bail and detainers

When a warrant, bail issue, probation detainer or missed hearing is involved, the first question is which court issued it, what triggered it and what step is needed to get back before the right judge. Bring the warrant notice, bail documents, probation documents and any scheduling notices.

Negotiation and trial preparation

Resolution may involve diversion, reduced charges, plea negotiations, trial or sentencing advocacy. The right path depends on the record, evidence, client goals and practical risk.

Collateral consequences

Criminal allegations can affect employment, licenses, education, immigration, custody, housing, military status and firearm rights. Those concerns should be raised early so they are considered before bail, plea, hearing and sentencing decisions are made.

Easton criminal defense FAQ

Questions people ask before contacting the office.

Should I talk to police if they say they only want my side?

Call an attorney before giving a statement. A conversation that seems informal may still affect an investigation, charging decision, bail issue or later testimony. If police are asking for a statement, identify the agency, officer, alleged incident and whether charges have already been filed.

What should I bring for a preliminary hearing review?

Bring the complaint, affidavit if available, summons, bail documents, hearing notice, witness information, messages, photos, video and anything showing your location, communications or the timeline of the alleged incident. If you received documents by mail, bring the envelope too.

Can a criminal case create work or licensing issues?

Yes. Employment, professional licensing, school, firearm, immigration, housing and family-law consequences can matter before a final disposition. Raise those concerns during the first conversation so they are not treated as an afterthought.

What if the hearing is very soon?

Contact the office and identify the court, date, time, judge or magisterial district judge, docket number and charges listed on the documents. Do not assume a continuance will be granted. Bring or send the court notice and charging documents as soon as possible.
