Custody issues

A custody plan needs facts, timing and practical proposals.

Most custody calls involve an immediate question: what schedule is in place, what court date is coming up, what changed, and what order is being requested. The same call may also involve concerns about school, medical care, transportation, communication, relocation, or safety.

County-specific procedure can matter in custody cases. Forms, service, conference scheduling, time-sensitive requests and hearing steps should be checked against the local court process. Procedure matters because a custody request may not be heard if service or scheduling is incomplete.

This page explains how the existing order, proposed schedule, dated examples, child-related facts and court notices can affect a filing, conference or hearing.

A custody case is not strengthened by volume alone. A few dated examples tied to school attendance, medical care, transportation, safety, exchanges, communication or the child's routine often matter more than pages of general frustration.

Write a short timeline.

Use dates and specific events. A focused custody timeline is often more helpful than a long unsorted history.

Helpful first details

  • County and court listed on the notice
  • Current custody order or schedule
  • Next conference, hearing or filing date
  • Requested schedule change and dated examples

Existing orders and current schedule

If there is an existing custody order, that order matters. If there is no written order, be ready to explain the schedule the child has actually been following, including exchange days, pickup times, drop-off locations, holidays, transportation and communication method.

Parenting schedule details

A proposed schedule should be specific enough to discuss. Identify weekdays, weekends, holidays, school breaks, transportation, exchanges, extracurricular activities, childcare, work schedules, and communication expectations.

Conference and hearing issues

Know the date, location, existing schedule and specific outcome being requested. Examples tied to the child's routine, school, health, safety, communication, transportation and parent-child relationship can help. A written proposal with actual times, locations and exchange logistics is often more helpful than a general request.

Relocation and schedule changes

Relocation and major schedule changes often require more than a preferred calendar. Distance, school placement, transportation, childcare, work schedules, housing, communication, extended family support and the child's routine may all matter. If relocation notice has been served or received, identify the service date, response date, hearing date and county listed.

School, medical and childcare issues

School attendance, report cards, teacher communications, medical information, therapy or counseling information, childcare, activity schedules, transportation and communications about appointments or school events may all matter when they show dates and context.

PFA, safety, and criminal-case overlap

Custody issues sometimes overlap with PFA matters, safety concerns, police reports, criminal charges, supervised exchanges or communication limits. If any of those issues exist, identify them during the first conversation. Do not send sensitive details by message before speaking with the office.

Conference and hearing issues

Preparing for a custody conference or hearing

Before a custody conference or hearing, focus on three things: the current schedule, the specific schedule you are requesting and the dated facts that explain why. Focus on the child's routine, school, health, safety, transportation, communication, and parent-child relationship.

Use a specific proposal

A proposal that names actual times, locations, and exchange logistics is generally more useful than a general request for more time or a broad label.

Be specific about what changed

A modification request often turns on what changed since the existing order. A short, dated list of changes is usually more useful than a general narrative.

Keep the focus on the child and the schedule

Specific dates, documents, examples, and child-related facts are easier to evaluate than general frustration about the relationship between adults.
