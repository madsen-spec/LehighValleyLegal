Custody preparation

A custody plan needs facts, timing and practical proposals.

Most custody calls begin with an immediate question: what schedule is in place, what court date is coming up, what changed, and what order is being requested. The same call may also involve concerns about school, medical care, transportation, communication, relocation, or safety.

County-specific procedure can matter in custody cases. Forms, service, conference scheduling, time-sensitive requests, and hearing preparation should be checked against the local court process. Procedure is part of the strategy because a custody request may not be heard if the documents, service, or scheduling step is incomplete.

This page helps organize the existing order, proposed schedule, dated examples, child-related records and court notices before the next filing, conference or hearing.

A custody case is not strengthened by volume alone. A few dated examples tied to school attendance, medical care, transportation, safety, exchanges, communication, or the child's routine are usually more useful than pages of general frustration.

Write a short timeline.

Use dates, events and documents. A focused custody timeline is usually more useful than a long unsorted history.

Useful first-call details

  • County and court listed on the notice
  • Current custody order or schedule
  • Next conference, hearing or filing date
  • Requested schedule change and dated examples

Existing orders and current schedule

Bring a complete copy of any existing custody order. If there is no written order, prepare a clear summary of the schedule the child has actually been following. Include regular exchange days, pickup times, drop-off locations, holidays, transportation arrangements, and communication method.

Parenting schedule details

A proposed schedule should be specific enough to discuss. Identify weekdays, weekends, holidays, school breaks, transportation, exchanges, extracurricular activities, childcare, work schedules, and communication expectations.

Conference and hearing preparation

Know the date, location, existing schedule, and specific outcome being requested. Prepare examples tied to the child's routine, school, health, safety, communication, transportation, and parent-child relationship. A written proposal with actual times, locations, and exchange logistics is usually more useful than a general request.

Relocation and schedule changes

Relocation and major schedule changes often require more than a preferred calendar. Bring information about distance, school placement, transportation, childcare, work schedules, housing, communication, extended family support, and how the proposed change would affect the child's routine. If relocation documents has been served or received, identify the service date, response date, hearing date, and county listed on the papers.

School, medical, and childcare records

Useful custody records may include school attendance, report cards, teacher communications, medical records, therapy or counseling records, childcare records, activity schedules, transportation information, and communications about appointments or school events. Bring records that show dates and context.

PFA, safety, and criminal-case overlap

Custody issues sometimes overlap with PFA matters, safety concerns, police reports, criminal charges, supervised exchanges, or communication limits. If any of those issues exist, identify them during the first conversation. Bring PFA papers, police reports, medical records, photos, messages, witness names, and hearing notices. Do not send sensitive details by message before speaking with the office.

Conference and hearing preparation

Preparing for a custody conference or hearing

Before a custody conference or hearing, organize three things: the current schedule, the specific schedule you are requesting, and the dated facts that explain why. Focus on the child's routine, school, health, safety, transportation, communication, and parent-child relationship.

Bring a written proposal

A proposal that names actual times, locations, and exchange logistics is generally more useful than a general request for more time or a broad label.

Be specific about what changed

A modification request often turns on what changed since the existing order. A short, dated list of changes is usually more useful than a general narrative.

Keep the focus on the child and the schedule

Specific dates, documents, examples, and child-related facts are easier to evaluate than general frustration about the relationship between adults.
