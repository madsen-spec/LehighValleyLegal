What to bring to a personal injury consultation.

A useful injury consultation connects the accident, proof, insurance and medical timeline. Bring organized records when possible, and contact the office directly if a release, filing date, recorded statement request or lawsuit paper has arrived.

Consultation packet

Bring records, not just a summary.

Personal injury evaluation depends on records that show what happened and how the injury affected health, work and daily life. A short written timeline is helpful, but photos, reports, medical documents and insurance correspondence usually matter more than memory alone.

If you do not have every record yet, bring what you have. The first evaluation can identify missing records, insurance questions and dates that should be checked next. Do not delay a call only because the police report, medical records or bills are incomplete.

Pennsylvania injury claims often use a two-year limitation period, but the actual deadline depends on the parties, facts and claim type. Do not delay if a public entity, minor, delayed diagnosis, workers' compensation overlap or unusual notice issue may be involved.

A simple folder structure can make the first meeting more productive. Separate accident records, medical records, insurance papers, wage-loss records, photos and questions. If records are digital, name files by date and type.

Do not sign before getting advice.

Release forms, settlement checks, medical authorizations and recorded statement requests can affect the claim. Save them and ask questions first.

Document checklist

Gather these before the first meeting.

Do not delay seeking guidance just because some records are missing. This checklist is meant to make the first discussion more focused.

Accident and scene records

Photos, videos, police reports, incident reports, property reports, repair estimates, vehicle photos and weather or condition notes. Include the date, location, agency, officer, incident number and names of people present when known.

Medical records and bills

Emergency room documents, discharge instructions, provider names, referrals, restrictions, prescriptions, bills, health insurance statements, therapy records and follow-up appointment information. Keep a list of all providers and treatment dates.

Insurance communications

Claim numbers, adjuster letters, messages, text messages, forms, recorded statement requests, authorizations, releases and settlement documents. Keep the envelope or message date when possible.

Work and daily-life impact

Pay stubs, missed work records, employer letters, restrictions, mileage, out-of-pocket expenses and notes about activity limitations. If a doctor limited work, lifting, driving or other activity, keep the written restriction.

How to organize

A simple folder structure works.

Put the accident report, photos, medical documents, bills, insurance letters and wage information in separate groups. If you use a digital folder, name files by date and document type. For paper records, keep the most recent letters first and avoid writing notes directly on original documents.

Bring a list of questions too. Common questions include whether to speak with an adjuster, how to get a report, whether medical bills should be submitted to health insurance, whether a release should be signed and what dates might matter.

If evidence may disappear, such as store video, vehicle data, dash camera footage, security footage or photos of a dangerous condition, identify that during the first conversation. Early preservation can matter.

Checklist FAQ

Injury consultation questions.

Should I wait until treatment is finished before contacting the office?

No. The claim may need early evidence preservation, deadline evaluation or insurance guidance even while treatment is ongoing. Treatment status can be updated later.

What if I do not know every medical provider yet?

Bring what you know now. A provider list can be updated as treatment continues. Include urgent care, hospital, family doctor, referral provider, therapy, imaging and pharmacy information.

Can I bring photos on my phone?

Yes, but preserve original files and do not delete metadata if possible. Make backup copies outside the phone and do not edit the original images.

What if the other insurance company sent a release?

Do not sign it until you understand what claims or records it covers. Bring the full release and any related letter to the consultation.
